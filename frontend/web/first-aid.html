01

Cuts and heavy bleeding

Uncontrolled bleeding is life-threatening within minutes. Act immediately to slow blood loss before transport.

  1. Apply firm direct pressure with a clean cloth or bandage and hold without lifting.
  2. Raise the injured area above heart level if it is safe to do so.
  3. Add more material on top if blood soaks through — do not remove the first layer.
  4. Seek urgent care if bleeding does not slow within 10 minutes.

02

Burns and scalds

Cooling a burn quickly reduces tissue damage and pain. Do not use ice, butter, or toothpaste.

  1. Cool the burn under clean running water for at least 20 minutes.
  2. Remove rings, watches, or tight clothing before swelling starts.
  3. Cover loosely with a clean non-stick dressing or cling film.
  4. Seek medical help for burns larger than a palm, on the face, or on a child.

03

Falls and suspected fractures

Moving a fracture incorrectly can cause additional injury. Immobilise and support until help arrives.

  1. Keep the person still and support the injured area in the position found.
  2. Apply a wrapped cold pack to reduce swelling — never directly on skin.
  3. Improvise a splint using a rigid object padded with cloth if transport is needed.
  4. Do not force movement if there is visible deformity or severe pain.

04

Choking

A blocked airway becomes fatal in minutes. Act fast and call emergency services immediately if the person cannot breathe.

  1. Encourage forceful coughing if the person can still breathe or speak.
  2. Lean them forward and give up to 5 firm back blows between the shoulder blades.
  3. If back blows fail, give up to 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre).
  4. Call emergency help immediately if breathing stops or the person loses consciousness.

05

Road accident or head injury

Spinal injuries are common in road accidents. Minimise movement and keep the scene safe until emergency services arrive.

  1. Make the scene safe — turn off engines and warn oncoming traffic if possible.
  2. Keep the person still and support the head and neck in the position found.
  3. Monitor breathing and visible bleeding while waiting for help.
  4. Do not remove helmets unless breathing is blocked and you must intervene.

06

Nosebleed

Most nosebleeds stop within 20 minutes with correct positioning and pressure. Avoid tilting the head back.

  1. Sit the person upright and lean slightly forward — not backward.
  2. Pinch the soft part of the nose firmly for 10 to 15 minutes without releasing.
  3. Breathe through the mouth and spit out any blood rather than swallowing.
  4. Seek help if bleeding is heavy, lasts beyond 20 minutes, or follows a head injury.

07

Fainting

Fainting is a brief loss of consciousness caused by reduced blood flow to the brain. Recovery is usually quick with correct positioning.

  1. Lay the person flat on their back and raise their legs above heart level.
  2. Loosen tight clothing around the neck and waist and allow fresh air.
  3. Do not give anything to eat or drink until fully conscious.
  4. Turn them onto their side if vomiting occurs or recovery is delayed beyond 2 minutes.

08

Electric shock

Never touch a person who may still be in contact with a live current. Your safety comes first.

  1. Switch off the power source at the mains before approaching the person.
  2. If the source cannot be switched off, use a dry non-conductive object to move them away.
  3. Check breathing and responsiveness once the area is confirmed safe.
  4. Seek urgent medical help even if the person appears stable — internal burns are not visible.

09

Poisoning

The correct response depends on the substance. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a medical professional.

  1. Remove the person from the source only if it is safe to do so.
  2. Do not give food, drink, or induce vomiting unless a medical professional advises it.
  3. Note the substance name, amount, and time of exposure for emergency services.
  4. Call emergency services or a poison control line immediately with the product details.

10

Animal bites

Animal bites carry infection risk including tetanus and rabies. Clean the wound thoroughly and seek medical review quickly.

  1. Wash the wound gently but thoroughly with clean running water and soap for 5 minutes.
  2. Control any bleeding with light direct pressure using a clean cloth.
  3. Cover with a clean dressing — do not close the wound tightly.
  4. Get medical review quickly to assess tetanus and rabies risk.
